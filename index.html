Design's Identity Crisis

The 1990s brought graphic design to a breaking point. As computers democratized the profession and consumer culture accelerated, designers found themselves torn between Modernist principles and postmodern chaos. Steven Heller's "Cult of the Ugly" (1993) attacked graduate school experiments as self-indulgent, warning that "ugliness as its own virtue diminishes all design." Anne Burdick's "Neomania" (1992) diagnosed the profession's addiction to novelty, admitting "we design to indulge our obsession with the visual." Ellen Lupton's "Under the Surface of Style" (1995) defended style as meaningful, calling it "an outward sign of difference." The contradiction was clear: designers preached function while competing on aesthetics, denounced formalism while reveling in it, and spoke of timeless principles while chasing constant reinvention. These essays capture a profession fighting over its identity when yesterday's innovation became today's cliché.

  • Neomania

    Anne Burdick diagnoses the profession's obsession with perpetual novelty, exposing the contradictions between design rhetoric and reality. She reveals how designers claim to follow functionalist principles while actually competing on aesthetics alone, trapped in a cycle where each new style is consumed and discarded overnight in the relentless pursuit of the next big thing.

  • Cult of the Ugly

    Steven Heller confronts what he sees as a dangerous trend: design schools producing deliberately ugly work in the name of experimentation. He argues that while ugliness can be valid when it serves a purpose, the current wave of chaotic, self-indulgent design threatens the profession's credibility and creates a cult of style without substance.

  • Under the Surface of Style

    Ellen Lupton challenges the dismissal of style as shallow and meaningless, arguing that it operates as a complex cultural code. She demonstrates how style communicates identity, taste, and social belonging, pushing back against Modernist binaries of form versus content to show that style is not decoration but a fundamental mode of communication in contemporary society.
